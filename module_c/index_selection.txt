
Alpha diversity quantifies the diversity of microbes within a single sample, encompassing both richness (number of different organisms) and evenness (how uniformly distributed those organisms are in terms of abundance). Beta diversity, in contrast, focuses on comparing species composition across samples (e.g., skin vs. digestive microbiome).

Alpha diversity metrics used in more than 65 microbiome studies were grouped into four categories following an exhaustive theoretical analysis (Cassol et al., 2025): 

richness: Chao1, ACE, Fisher, Margalef, Menhinick, Observed, and Robbins 
evenness: Berger-Parker, Dominance, Simpson, ENSPIE, Gini, McIntosh, and Strong 
phylogenetics: Faith
information: Shannon, Brillouin, Heip, and Pielou.

The analyses revealed that many of these metrics are highly correlated, meaning that greater formula complexity does not necessarily yield more informative results. The authors therefore recommend reporting at least one metric per category, prioritizing the simplest option with behavior representative of its counterparts (Cassol et al., 2025).

Accordingly, the following metrics were selected. For richness, Observed features was chosen for its simplicity and intuitive biological interpretation, supplemented by the Robbins value, which estimates the probability of not observing taxa present in the sample. For evenness, Simpson's dominance was used to capture the degree to which a community is dominated by its most abundant taxa, as Berger-Parker is unavailable in QIIME 2, Simpson serves as its closest equivalent. For phylogenetics, Faith's Phylogenetic Diversity was reported. For information, Shannon entropy was selected as it jointly summarizes both richness and evenness in a single value.

For beta diversity, Bray–Curtis and similar non-compositional distances are more sensitive to dominant taxa when abundance is highly skewed, whereas Weighted UniFrac accounts for abundance differences and phylogenetic relationships, giving a more balanced view across dominant and rare taxa (Fuschi et al., 2025).
